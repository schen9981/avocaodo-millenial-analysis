Project Overview and Hypothesis

This projects seeks to explore the trends in avocado consumption in the US, in connection with millenials, a group classified as those born between 1980 and 1994.

Hypothesis: We expect that cities in the United States with a higher proportion of millenials consume more avocados despite prices increases in recent years.

Methodology

Our analysis consisted of 3 main components:

  1. Reorganization and Joining Data

    After finding and cleaning our raw data and reading them into database files, we reorganized, joined, and did further cleaning in order to best support our analysis methods

  2. Statistical Tests

    This entailed computing relevant metrics, statistical tests to determine the strength of the relationship of our variables, and regressions. For this checkpoint, we ran some simple OLS regressions.

  3. Visualizations

    We plotted the raw data, as well as different metrics that would help us gauge general trends over the years. We also visualized the outputs of our regression models.

Reorganization and Joining Data

Our analysis was dependent on common regions (California, Plains, South Central, Midsouth, Southeast, Great Lakes, Northeast) defined by the Hass Avocado Board for both the avocado consumption data and the demographic data.

Our raw data composed of the avocado consumption for 2016, 2017, and 2018 for cities/states specified in the raw data, as well as the accumulated consumption data for Hass Avocado Board defined regions. Thus, we extracted out the accumulated consumption for the regions specified above, into a new table.

As for our raw data for demographic, this had data regarding gender, age, and population for each state for each of the years 2016, 2017, and 2018. We first removed the data for Alaska and Hawaii, as these states were not taken into account for the avocado consumption regions. We created a new column called 'region', where we labeled each state with its appropriate region. Then, for each state in region X, we added up population for each age (while keeping gender separated).

This way, we have a 'region' parameter across our demographic and avocado consumption data that we then used to join the tables and perform analysis.

We also used population data to compute and store the per capita avocado sales for each region. Running tests on per capita data makes more sense then running tests on lump-sum data because each region has a different number of consumers, which is a confounding variable when analyzing the relationship between the proportion of millenials and number of avocados sold.

Statistical Tests

We used a simple linear regression because it served as a way to see the extent of effect our independent variable (millennial population percentage) had on our dependent variable (avocados sold per capita), and in what way our input variable impacted the output variable. We considered running a two-sample t-Test, but we had trouble determining which 2 samples, given our current data sets, we would run it on in order to test our hypothesis. Success would be measured by observing the R-squared value and the regression coefficient, as a relatively high R-squared value provides an estimate of the strength of the relationship between our model and the dependent variable and a positive regression coefficient would indicate that as the value of the independent variable increases, the mean of the dependent variable also tends to increase.

One challenge we faced while evaluating the model was since our initial data points consisted of 8 regions, it would be hard to control appropriately. Therefore, we may need to restructure our data by trying to get city-level data and then expand our analysis accordingly.

A summary of our linear regression run on each year is below: (2016, 2017, 2018)

Based on the simple linear regression and the R-squared values, we can see that the variability in millennial population percentage explains a good portion of the variability in the per capita avocados sold data around its mean. Additionally, a positive regression coeffeicient indicates that as our independent variable increases, the mean of the dependent variable also tends to increase.

One challenge of our project is controlling for confounding variables. Consumer preferences are influenced by a ton of different factors, and age is only one of them. It's also tough to control for variables when our regions cover such large areas.

We're hoping to analyze these factors more thoroughly in the coming weeks, ideally by finding more fine-grained region data. For now, our tests are more suitable for testing correlation than causation. Perhaps avocado sales are closely tied with disposable income levels and millenials happen to have the highest level of disposable income. Or, pehaps avocado sales are closely linked with the prevalence of "health-campaigns" advertising the fruit as a super-food and millenials happen to be most-targetted. Either way, we think our tests offers some support to our hypothesis, although we can't necessarily claim a causal relationship.
